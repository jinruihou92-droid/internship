Finding the placement

An internship I
designed myself

I didn't find this internship through a job board. I created it. In the summer of 2025, I noticed a gap I kept returning to: while my thesis wants to focus on death education and emotion regulation, I had never actually talked to ordinary people — not students, not academics — about how they personally think and feel about death.

So I designed a qualitative interview study from scratch. I reached out to a cultural consulting organisation whose mission aligned with cross-cultural communication and content research, and we agreed to frame the project as an official internship placement. The work was independently initiated and self-directed — but grounded in an organisational framework that gave it structure and accountability.

In terms of logistics: the project ran from May to July 2025, entirely during the summer break, so it didn't overlap with the teaching period. This made scheduling flexible — but also required strong self-discipline to stay on track without external deadlines.

How the work unfolded

Four phases,
one complete cycle

01
Research design & recruitment
May 2025 · ~25 hours

Designed a 30-question semi-structured interview guide across 5 themes. Published a recruitment post on Xiaohongshu (Rednote) and confirmed 30 participants.

02
Conducting interviews
May–June 2025 · ~60 hours

Ran 30 interviews — 2 in-person, 28 online. Participants ranged from students abroad to working adults in China. One interview with a British-French teacher in Finland.

03
Transcription & analysis
June 2025 · ~50 hours

Transcribed all recordings and conducted thematic analysis. Identified patterns across cultural backgrounds, generations, and personal experience.

04
Content & publishing
June–July 2025 · ~45 hours

Scripted, filmed, edited and published multiple posts on Xiaohongshu. Engaged with audience responses. Bridged research findings and public storytelling.

Interview guide structure

Five themes that
opened conversations

01

Formative encounters with death

When did you first become aware of death? What shaped your earliest understanding of it?

02

Farewell & loss experiences

What was the most profound goodbye you have experienced? How did it change you?

03

Cultural rituals & taboos

How does your culture handle funerals, grave-sweeping, and mourning? What is spoken — and what isn't?

04

Personal fears

What scares you most about death? The moment itself, or what comes after — or neither?

05

End-of-life imagination

If you had one week left to live, what would you do? What kind of funeral would you want?

Learning objectives

What the LET programme
made possible

This project would not have been feasible without LET training. The methods I had studied in Qualitative Research Methodology and Quantitative Research Methodology gave me the analytical scaffolding to design, execute and interpret a real study — not just an exercise.

The LET learning objectives around emotion regulation, critical thinking, cross-cultural understanding, and technology-mediated communication were directly activated. I used digital platforms both to recruit participants and to disseminate findings — experiencing firsthand how research and technology intersect in public-facing work.

This internship gave it an empirical layer: I have 30 interviews worth of real-world data that will continue to inform my thinking, and it also provides me with an empirical backdrop which lays a theoretical foundation for my master's thesis, which is on death education and emotion regulation.

◈
Qualitative methods in practice

Semi-structured interviewing and thematic analysis moved from coursework exercises to lived practice. I learned to be flexible within a framework — adapting questions to participants while maintaining research rigour.

◈
Ethics with sensitive topics

Recruiting participants online for conversations about death required careful ethical design. I developed protocols for informed consent and emotional safety that went beyond what coursework had prepared me for.

◈
Research-to-storytelling translation

Turning nuanced interview data into accessible Xiaohongshu content meant making difficult editorial choices. This creative-analytical tension was one of the most valuable skills I built.

◈
Self-directed project management

Without a supervisor setting daily tasks, I had to develop systems for managing recruitment, scheduling, data storage and content production — all simultaneously, across 175 hours.

Career relevance

How this shapes
what comes next

I am considering a career at the intersection of qualitative research, cultural communication, and public education — and this project confirmed that this is where my skills and interests converge.

Having a publicly visible body of work on Xiaohongshu — accessible to thousands of readers — means I can point to concrete outputs when applying for research or communications roles. The project also deepened my connection to my thesis topic, which I believe will make me a stronger candidate for doctoral study or research positions in the future.

More broadly: this internship taught me that I am capable of designing and executing a full research cycle independently. That confidence is perhaps the most transferable thing I take away.

For next year's students

What I wish
someone had told me

01
You can create your own placement

If you have a research idea or a project you've been meaning to pursue, consider whether it can be framed as an internship. Self-initiated projects require more discipline but offer far more freedom — and the outputs belong entirely to you.

02
Your coursework is more useful than you think

I used qualitative research methods I had learned in class directly, in a real study, within weeks of the course ending. Don't underestimate what your LET training has given you.

03
Sensitive topics require ethical care — but they're worth it

Recruiting strangers to talk about death felt daunting at first. But people are often grateful for the space to reflect. If your research touches something real in people's lives, they will show up for it.

04
Document everything from day one

Working hours, participant notes, decisions you made and why. You will need all of it later — for the portfolio, for your own learning, and for any future work you want to build on this experience.

05
Let the internship connect to everything else

The most valuable internships are not separate from your studies — they're the place where your coursework, your thesis, and your career direction all meet at once. Look for that convergence point.
